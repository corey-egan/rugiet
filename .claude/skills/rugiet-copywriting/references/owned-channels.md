## How to use this doc

This guide pairs with [Tone of Voice](./tone-of-voice.md) (the authoritative tone-of-voice document). TOV establishes who Rugiet is, who we talk to, and how we talk. This doc translates that voice into the specific operating logic for two owned channels: **email** and **website**.

Owned channels are surfaces Rugiet controls, speaking to audiences who already know the brand — customers and warm leads. That permission changes the job. We don't need to earn attention with hooks or theater. We earn loyalty with clarity, credibility, and matter-of-fact directness.

If anything in this doc appears to contradict TOV, defer to TOV.

---

## Voice calibration for owned channels

TOV sets the baseline voice across all of Rugiet. Owned channels move the dials in specific directions.

**Dial up:**
- Education and clinical credibility
- Plain medical language ("ED," "PE," "testosterone," "sildenafil," "sex")
- Specificity (numbers, mechanisms, regimens)
- Practical information the reader can act on

**Dial down:**
- Humor as a tactic — especially in email (see hard nos below)
- Provocation for its own sake
- Double-entendres and wordplay
- Hooks engineered for attention rather than service

**Hold steady at TOV baseline:**
- Directness — say what we mean, in as few words as possible
- Confidence — assume the sale, never beg or convince
- The dinner-party-guy archetype — done well, knows it, doesn't announce it
- Let the product and the doctors speak

**Customer mindset rule:** The reader is not a broken patient. The reader is an aspirational mirror — a guy who has standards and has been underserved by the category. Write to that man. The emotional contract underneath every owned-channel piece: **you haven't peaked, you've been underserved.**

## Shared rules across both channels

### Formatting
- Sentence case throughout — headlines, subheads, buttons, CTAs
- No exclamation points
- Oxford commas
- No colons
- Contractions are natural and encouraged
- Em dash: one per copy block maximum, never used as a casual comma
- Single space after periods
- Numbers: spell out one through nine, numerals for 10 and above, numerals for all medical and dosage values ("5mg tablet," not "5 mg tablet")

### Sentence structure
- Active voice
- One clear thought per sentence
- Short sentences. Short paragraphs.
- Cut qualifiers — "extremely effective" becomes "effective"
- Lead with the strongest statement

### Language hard nos
- No alliteration used as a substitute for substance
- No "just checking in," "wanted to follow up," or other filler
- No fabricated doctor quotes (compliance hard rule, applies everywhere)
- No corporate jargon or buzzwords

### Medical content
- Drug names lowercase in body copy ("sildenafil"); capitalized only for the specific branded formulation
- Dosage formatting clinical ("5mg," not "5 mg")
- Side effect lists use parallel structure

### Compliance baseline (both channels)
- Use conditional verbs: *can, may, helps, designed to, supports, formulated to*
- "Up to" before duration claims
- "Individual results may vary" on any testimonial content
- No "guaranteed" or "miracle" anywhere
- Comparative claims must be substantiated

## Email

### Role in the mix
Email is where Rugiet educates, builds credibility, and drives action from audiences who already opted in. It's the heaviest educational-lifting channel in the system. It's also the most controlled — no algorithm to game, no platform compliance to dodge. That means we can be more directly explicit about health topics in email than in paid social.

The voice in email is punchy and direct, but the primary job is **education, not entertainment**. Save wit for channels where it serves acquisition.

### Email types and voice calibration

| Type | Examples | Voice calibration |
|---|---|---|
| **Marketing campaigns** | Promos, content drops, product launches, newsletters | Most brand-voicey of the three. Educational lean. Single point. One CTA. |
| **Lifecycle flows** | Welcome, abandoned cart, refill reminders, win-back, post-purchase | Warm and contextually aware. Speak directly to the moment the customer is in. |
| **Transactional** | Order confirmations, shipping updates, account updates, password resets | Most stripped-down. Functional clarity over voice. Brand shows up in word choices ("provider," "pharmacy partner," "regimen"), not in being clever. |

### Format conventions
Every email follows this structure.

**Subject line:**
- Six words or fewer wherever possible
- Sentence case
- No terminal punctuation unless integral to meaning
- States clear value or creates curiosity — never clickbait
- Delivers on whatever the line promises
- Examples: "Your treatment plan is ready" / "Refill window opens Friday" / "Three things your doctor wants you to know"

**Preview text:**
- Punctuated as a complete sentence
- Extends the subject — never repeats it
- Functions as the email's second line of attack

**Delivery format when drafting for review:**

```
S: [subject line]

Prev: [preview text]

[body]
```

**Body copy:**
- Lead with the point in line one — do not bury it
- One idea per email — if there are two ideas, split into two emails
- Paragraphs no longer than three to four lines
- White space is a feature
- Bold key takeaways sparingly to help scanning
- Cut every sentence that doesn't earn its place

**Call-to-action:**
- One CTA per email
- Active, specific language: "Refill your prescription," "See your results," "Update your shipping"
- Never "Click here" or "Learn more"

### Email-specific hard nos
- **No puns.** Including product-name puns. This is the buttoned-up channel.
- **No idiomatic expressions.** "No shame in the game." "Get back in the saddle." "On the up and up." All cut.
- **No humor tactics.** Humor dilutes credibility here.
- **No multi-sentence hooks.** The hook is one tight sentence.
- **No multiple competing CTAs.**
- **No "just checking in"** or any variant of feigned passive interest.
- **No fabricated doctor quotes** — applies everywhere, but worth restating.

### Email compliance notes
Owned channels permit more directness about health topics than paid does. In email, the following are acceptable:
- "You have ED" / "Your testosterone is low" / "You've gone soft" / "You can't get an erection"
- Naming specific symptoms and conditions plainly

Still required in email:
- Conditional verbs on all benefit claims
- Compliant onset language ("works in as little as 15 minutes")
- Duration claims capped with "up to"
- Disclaimers on any testimonial content ("Customer's results have not been independently verified. Individual results may vary.")
- Actor portrayal disclosure if a paid actor appears
- No absolute or guaranteed claims

### Product-specific email rules
- **Go Long:** PE is the marquee benefit. Tadalafil's ED support may appear in body copy but never as the lead. Do not put "ED" in subject lines for Go Long.
- **TRT:** Lead with personalization — doctor-designed, built around your biology, your levels, your goals — not with lab testing. Personalization is Rugiet's strongest TRT message.
- **Longevity (NAD+, Sermorelin, Lipo-C, L-Carnitine, Glutathione):** Operate in optimization register, not deficiency register. Normal is the enemy, not low. Frame around upgrade and capability, not lack and decline.
- **Rugiet Ready:** Onset claim language is precise. Approved compliant phrasing: "works in as little as 15 minutes." Never "works in as fast as."

## Website

### Role in the mix
The website is where intent gets converted. Each surface has a distinct job.

| Surface | Job |
|---|---|
| **PDPs** | Convert. Explain the product, the why, the how, the proof. |
| **Category pages** | Orient. Frame the category, help the right product surface for the right need. |
| **Homepage** | Frame the brand. Establish POV, signal breadth of offering, drive to action. |
| **Account portal** | Support existing customers. Functional clarity above brand voice. |

### PDP architecture
This is the established Rugiet PDP structure. Every product follows it.

**Section 1: Above the fold**
- Category tag (Sexual Health, TRT, Longevity, Sleep, Weight Loss, Hair)
- Product name
- Parallax left-side copy: three short benefit lines, each paired with an image
- Quick facts: three to four plain factual bullets (what it is, how it's delivered, what it supports)
- "What is [product]?" — clinical definition in two to three sentences
- "How do I take it?" — practical regimen, deferred to provider
- "Common side effects" — honest, balanced, deferred to provider

**Section 2: What's happening in your body**
- Mechanism-focused
- Typically two numbered explainers
- Plain language, no textbook tone

**Section 3: The 'why' — three buckets**
- Headline and sub-headline
- Three parallel lists:
  - How it can look
  - How it can feel
  - How it can impact performance

**Section 4: Comparison chart**
- Rugiet vs. supplements, or Rugiet vs. conventional alternative
- Earned by a sharp headline

**Section 5: Bold text block**
- Anchor statement that captures the product's core promise

**Section 6: How it works**
- The customer-facing process flow

**Section 7: Clinical credibility**
- Doctor-guided, pharmacy-sourced, Rx-required — the primary trust lever for the brand
- Real physicians where applicable

**Section 8: Cross-sell**
- Adjacent or complementary products

**Section 9: FAQ**
- Direct, plain, answers the actual question in line one

### Category pages
- Open with a category-level POV — what Rugiet thinks about this category and why we're in it
- Surface products with brief differentiators
- Help the visitor self-route to the right PDP
- Voice: same calibration as PDP — educational, credible, no humor tactics

### Homepage
- The most brand-voicey surface in this group
- Establishes the Rugiet POV: men's healthcare has a personality problem; Rugiet is the opposite
- Standard architecture: hero, value props, social proof, breadth-of-offering, CTA
- Headlines can be a beat sharper than PDP headlines — provocation is permitted when it meets the TOV boldness test: *is there something true here that nobody else is willing to say?*
- Provocation is aimed at conventions, ideas, and lies — never at the customer

### Account portal
- Functional clarity comes first — this is closest to UX register
- Brand voice shows up in word choices and tone, never in being clever
- Examples:
  - ✅ "Your next refill ships November 14."
  - ❌ "Locked and loaded — your next refill is on its way."
  - ✅ "Your provider has updated your dose. Review the change."
  - ❌ "Big news from your doctor."
- Keep the matter-of-fact, adult register from TOV — dial down the provocation and humor

### Website-specific rules
- All headers sentence case
- Bold sparingly — white space is the primary emphasis tool
- Standard bullets, no decorative characters
- Headlines do not take terminal punctuation unless absolutely necessary
- Buttons and CTAs sentence case ("Get started," not "Get Started")

### Website compliance notes
Same baseline as email, plus:
- Comparison charts require substantiation on every comparative claim
- Clinical credibility section must accurately reflect doctor and pharmacy partners
- Testimonials flagged with "Customer's results have not been independently verified. Individual results may vary."
- "FDA-approved" only used for products that are. Compounded products are labeled accurately as not FDA-approved.

### Product-specific website rules
- **Go Long PDP:** PE is the marquee. Headline, category positioning, and above-the-fold copy lead PE. The ED benefit is body-copy-only and never appears in the hero.
- **TRT PDP:** Personalization first. Doctor-guided plans built around the patient's biology — that's the lead. Lab testing is a feature, not the headline.
- **Longevity PDPs (NAD+, Sermorelin, Lipo-C, L-Carnitine, Glutathione):** Optimization register. Headlines frame upgrade and capability — "Cellular energy, recharged" / "Stronger from the inside out" / "Fat that won't budge" — not deficiency or correction.
- **Sexual Health PDPs (Ready, Go Long, Daily Boost, Grower):** Plain language about sex and erections. The category is direct.

## Common failure modes to catch

Run through these before delivering output.

1. **Pun or wordplay in an email.** Almost always wrong here. Cut.
2. **Multi-sentence email hook.** The hook is one tight sentence.
3. **"Click here" or "Learn more" as a CTA.** Replace with active, specific language.
4. **Headline ending in a period or other punctuation.** Only when truly necessary.
5. **Absolute or guaranteed claims.** Add conditional verbs or remove the claim.
6. **"Works in as fast as."** Wrong phrasing. Use "works in as little as."
7. **Fabricated doctor quote.** Never. Use real quotes from real providers or cut entirely.
8. **Multiple CTAs in one email.** Pick one.
9. **Provocation aimed at the customer.** The enemy is the lie, the assumption, the convention — never the man.
10. **Account portal copy that's too clever.** Strip the personality if it gets in the way of clarity.
11. **"ED" leading a Go Long communication.** PE is the marquee. ED is body copy only.
12. **TRT copy that leads with lab testing.** Personalization first.
13. **Longevity copy in deficiency register.** Frame optimization, not lack.
14. **Exclamation point.** Anywhere. Ever. Cut.

## Self-check before delivering output

Run this checklist before returning copy.

1. **Channel match.** Does the calibration fit the specific surface — campaign vs. flow vs. transactional, PDP vs. homepage vs. category vs. portal?
2. **Voice match.** Does this sound like the dinner-party-guy archetype — confident, direct, matter-of-fact, adult? Or has it slid into pharma textbook, punning sales bro, or wellness-influencer cliché?
3. **Format match.** Subject line under six words? Sentence case throughout? No exclamation? Single CTA? PDP sections in order? Headlines without terminal punctuation?
4. **Compliance check.** Conditional verbs on benefit claims? Disclaimers where required? No absolute or guaranteed language? "As little as" not "as fast as"? Channel-appropriate directness (plain medical language is fine in owned channels)?
5. **Hard nos clear.** No puns in email? No idiomatic expressions? No clichés? No fabricated doctor quotes? No filler transitions?
6. **TOV alignment.** If the copy provokes, does it pass the test — *is there something true here that nobody else is willing to say?* Is the provocation aimed at an idea or convention, never at a person?
7. **Customer-mindset check.** Does this address the reader as an aspirational mirror — a man who has standards and has been underserved — rather than as a broken patient who needs sympathy?
8. **Product positioning check.** Are product-specific rules respected (Go Long PE-first, TRT personalization-first, Longevity optimization register, Ready onset language)?

If anything fails, fix before delivering.
