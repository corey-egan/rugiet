---

## The point of view that starts everything

Men's healthcare has a personality problem. It's either clinical to the point of coma, or so wrapped in euphemisms you'd think "erection" was a curse word. Supplements promise everything, deliver nothing, and somehow keep selling. Providers tell men to "wait and see" like the body's on its own timeline and they should be patient about it. The whole category treats men like they should be grateful for vague answers and half-measures.

We think that's embarrassing. And we think most of it is on purpose.

The vagueness sells. The shame sells. The "manage your expectations" speech sells. There's an entire industry built on the assumption that if you confuse a man enough, or shame him enough, he'll settle for less and pay for it twice.

Rugiet exists to call that out and replace it. Men's health should work the way men actually want things to work: say what it is, say what fixes it, skip the bullshit, get it done. If the medicine works, prove it. If you're talking about sex, say sex. If a man asks a direct question, give him a direct answer. This isn't complicated. Everyone else just made it complicated because complicated is easier to hide behind.

We're not hiding behind anything. We're being direct with guys, with real information and real treatments that deliver on what they need.

## Who we are in the room

Rugiet is the guy at the dinner party who's done well, looks good, knows it, and doesn't need to tell you. He has real confidence, which means he's comfortable talking about things other men avoid.

He's also the guy who's not afraid to be provocative. Not loudly, not for attention. When the table has settled into a comfortable agreement that turns out to be a lie, he's the one who says it. Sex after fifty. Testosterone. Sleep. Performance. He's not afraid to set the record straight or push back against the so-called conventional wisdom.

He's been around long enough to know two things. One: most of what men are told to tolerate, they don't have to. Two: the people telling them to tolerate it usually have something to gain.

He's not a doctor. But he knows exactly which doctor to call, why that one, and what to ask. He's done the homework. He hands you the answer.

## The guy we're talking to

He's 45 to 65. He's built something — a career, a family, a life that matters. Lately, some part of that life has slipped. Maybe it's in the bedroom. Maybe it's energy, sleep, drive. He's noticed the gap between how he feels and how he knows he's capable of feeling.

He doesn't want sympathy. He wants someone to look him in the eye, give him the facts, and step aside while he handles it.

He's been told, in a hundred small ways, to expect less. To accept the slide. To be grateful for what's left. We do the opposite. We remind him to expect more — and we hand him the tools to get it.

## When we get bold

This is the section that keeps us sharp. A brand without edges is a brand people scroll past.

We get bold when there's a convention worth breaking. When the category is whispering, we speak at normal volume. When competitors sell shame, we sell standards. When the culture tells a man he should accept less at fifty-five than he expected at thirty-five, we ask who benefits from him believing that. When the standard line is "this is just aging," we ask what's actually true, and who decided we should stop questioning it.

We pick fights with ideas, never people. We don't punch down at customers, we don't turn men into the butt of the joke. The enemy is always the lie, the assumption, the dusty convention — never the guy in front of us.

And we earn it. Boldness without authority is just noise. Every sharp line we write is backed by real medicine, real doctors, real outcomes. That's what separates a brand with a point of view from a brand with an attitude.

**The question to ask before publishing something provocative:** is there something true here that nobody else is willing to say? If yes, say it. If it's just loud, cut it.

## How we talk

**We're direct.** We say what we mean in as few words as we can. There's warmth underneath, because we respect the guy reading. But we don't pad, soften, or apologize our way to the point.

**We're clinical when it counts.** Real medical authority is the foundation. We use words like ED, testosterone, sildenafil, serotonin the way a well-read friend uses them — conversationally, accurately, without making it feel like a textbook.

**We're matter-of-fact about the stuff other brands tiptoe around.** Sex. Hormones. Aging. Performance. We talk about all of it plainly. The topic doesn't need theater, it needs a solution.

**We provoke on purpose.** When the category has accepted something it shouldn't have — that decline is normal, that "wait and see" is care, that euphemism is decency — we say so. We do it with confidence, not heat. We do it with specifics, not slogans. The provocation is always pointed at the lie, not at the man. And it's always backed by something true: a stat, a study, a clinical fact, an outcome. Without that, it's just noise. With it, it's the reason men choose us over everyone else still whispering.

**We assume the sale.** Our tone says: of course you'd address this. Of course you'd want the better option. We don't beg or convince. We lay out the case and trust him.

**We let the product speak.** Our medicine works, our doctors are real, the outcomes are measurable. That earns us the right to be plain, and it earns us the right to be bold.

## How humor works

Humor is a tool we use carefully, not a personality we lean on. It's the knowing wink, the double-entendre that lands a beat after you read it, the line that makes a grown man smirk.

The reference is 70s Playboy editorial. Cultured, confident, adult. A man comfortable enough with sex and himself to find the humor without becoming a caricature.

Puns, boner jokes, college-bro wordplay — those are for brands that need humor to carry the product. Our product carries itself. Humor is the seasoning.

**The test:** would a confident fifty-year-old smirk at this or roll his eyes? Smirk means it's working.

## The emotional contract

Underneath everything — the copy, the positioning, the clinical language — we're doing one thing: we're telling men that their best years aren't behind them. That the version of themselves they remember is an option, not a memory. That addressing what's slipped isn't weakness, it's the move of a man who still has standards.

The undercurrent of every Rugiet communication: **you haven't peaked. You've just been underserved. Now you're not.**
